Philosophy

Fast models, slow engineering.

The name “System2” is borrowed from Daniel Kahneman, and most descriptions stop at the borrowing. But the name is not a mood. It is a specific, falsifiable claim about what today’s coding agents are, what they get wrong, and what it takes to fix them.

Planning is the work of the intellect

Reason at its most ambitious is patient. The pursuits we most admire—mathematics and medicine, architecture and law, the long accretion of scientific discovery—are triumphs of the deliberate mind: of foresight, of revision, of holding a goal steady across months and years. In Kahneman’s dual-process account, this is System 2 at work—the slow track: deliberate, sequential, reflective, the part of us that drafts and redrafts, saves for next winter, sleeps on a hard decision, and checks the sum a second time.1 Its partner, System 1, is the fast track: automatic, associative, effortless, always on—how we catch a falling glass, read a face, and finish the phrase “bread and …”. We lean on both every ordinary day we plan past this afternoon.

If System 1 is something like the Id—quick, instinctive, hungry for the answer now—then System 2 is the rational ego that gives those impulses a plan and a horizon. The two are partners, not rivals: intuition proposes, deliberation composes. We named the framework System2 after the faculty we most want to lend to building with AI—not to slow the fast mind down, but to give it the long view it does its best work inside.

What System 1 actually is

System 1 is not stupid. It is usually right, which is precisely what makes it dangerous. Its errors are not random noise; they are systematic, and they have names.2

  • WYSIATI—“what you see is all there is.” System 1 builds the most coherent story it can from the information in front of it, and is blind to what is missing. Confidence flows from the coherence of the story, not from the quality of the evidence.3
  • Attribute substitution. Faced with a hard question (“is this architecture correct?”), System 1 quietly answers an easier one (“does this look like correct architecture?”) and reports the answer to the question it was actually asked.4
  • Cognitive ease mistaken for truth. Information that is fluent, familiar, and easy to process simply feels true. Fluency is read as validity.1
  • The lazy controller. Because checking is effortful, System 2 mostly defers—rubber-stamping System 1 rather than auditing it.1

A modern LLM is a magnificent System 1

A language model is, in architecture, a fast associative pattern-matcher trained to produce the most plausible continuation of its context. That is a near-perfect mechanical analogue of System 1—and it inherits System 1’s failure modes almost line for line.

  • WYSIATI becomes acting on the prompt and the visible context as if they were the whole problem—confidently filling gaps with whatever makes the story cohere, and never raising the requirement that was simply never stated.
  • Substitution becomes producing code that looks like a solution in place of code that is one: the “looks done” diff, the test that asserts the function ran rather than that it is correct.
  • Cognitive ease as truth is the central hazard. A language model is a fluency engine; fluency is the one property it is guaranteed to deliver. It will explain a wrong answer as smoothly and as confidently as a right one—and the prose reads as correct for exactly the reason Kahneman warns about.
  • The lazy controller becomes the harness default. One-shot generation is cheap; deliberation costs tokens, latency, and turns. So the path of least resistance—for the model and for the product built around it—is fast, confident, unchecked output.

This is the part the soundbite misses. The trouble with agent-assisted coding is not that the model is dim. It is that the model is a brilliant intuition with no native faculty for doubting itself—and we keep wrapping it in harnesses that reward the intuition and skip the doubt.

The economics of immediate gratification

Why do harnesses skip the doubt? Because every incentive points at the present moment. A fast answer gratifies now: the demo runs, the test goes green, the diff appears. Verification, planning, and written specifications pay off later—in the bug that never ships, the rewrite that never happens, the page that never fires. This is intertemporal choice, and the fast system discounts the future steeply in favour of the reward in hand.5

Default LLM harnesses optimise for the immediate reward: the next token, the next passing check, the change that looks done this turn. It is the marshmallow test, run on machines—and most harnesses eat the marshmallow.6 The cost is deferred, not avoided. It returns as the integration that does not hold, the security hole nobody modelled, the “working” feature that encodes a requirement no one actually agreed to. System 2 work is the opposite trade: slower and dearer up front, buying down risk you would otherwise pay for downstream at a worse exchange rate.

You cannot prompt a System 1 into being a System 2

Here is the move that separates System2 from “just ask the model to think step by step.”

In a human, System 2 is a faculty inside the same head—limited and lazy, but present. A language model has no such second system to mobilise. Instructing it to “be careful,” “think hard,” or “double-check” recruits more of the same fast, fluent pattern-matching; it produces text about deliberation, which is itself a System 1 product and just as prone to confident coherence. The reasoning a model narrates need not be the computation that produced its answer.7 And asking the same model to grade its own work is the lazy controller endorsing System 1—models will sooner agree with you, or with themselves, than mount a genuine challenge.8

So deliberation has to be externalised—built as a process around the model, the way humans extend their own limited System 2 with tools that live outside the head: writing things down, doing the arithmetic on paper, working a checklist, asking a colleague to review. Kahneman’s own remedies for bias are all external scaffolding—the pre-mortem, reference-class forecasting, structured decision hygiene—precisely because you cannot will System 1 into rigour from the inside.9

You can’t prompt a fast mind into a slow one. The second system has to be built around it — in specs, gates, and review — not asked for.

How System2 externalises the second system

Each mechanism in the pipeline maps to a specific antidote for a specific System 1 failure.

Specs before code

Scope → Context → Requirements → Design → Tasks. Forcing articulation before generation is a debiasing technique. A written, versioned specification is the cure for WYSIATI and substitution: the implicit requirement must be made explicit before there is any fluent diff to be seduced by. Requirements stated as testable contracts are a near-literal pre-mortem—naming what the work must satisfy before the work exists to flatter you.9

Quality gates

Gates are deliberately introduced friction. System 1 wants to flow from idea to “done”; a gate interrupts the flow and forces a slow check at the point where a fast mistake is cheapest to catch. This is decision hygiene: a stop, on purpose, before an irreversible step.

Generation separated from verification

A distinct code reviewer, security sentinel, and test engineer are external monitors—System 2’s actual job, watching System 1, handed to a different agent with a different mandate and fresh context, so the verifier is not the same intuition that wrote the code grading its own homework.

Subagents with fresh context

Division of cognitive labour, and a defence against anchoring: a reviewer that did not live through the generation is not primed by it, and is freer to disagree.2

None of this makes the underlying model less of a System 1. It surrounds the System 1 with the structure a deliberate engineer would impose on themselves—and makes that structure the contract rather than a suggestion.

The honest tension

A narrative that only praised slow thinking would be committing System 1’s mistake in the other direction. Kahneman is emphatic that System 1 is indispensable and usually correct; a mind that ran everything through System 2 would seize up, and so would an engineering process. Deliberation has real costs—latency, tokens, ceremony, the risk of turning judgment into bureaucracy. Friction applied indiscriminately does not produce rigour; it produces theatre that people learn to click through.

The goal, then, is not maximum deliberation. It is calibrated deliberation: effort proportional to the stakes and, above all, to irreversibility. Spend System 2 where a mistake is expensive or hard to undo—architecture, security boundaries, public interfaces, data migrations—and let fast intuition run where it is cheap and easily corrected: exploration, scaffolding, throwaway spikes. System 1 is great for exploration and risky as the only mode for production changes. Gates should be sized to the blast radius, not stamped uniformly on everything. A good harness makes the slow path available, and the default where it matters—not mandatory everywhere.

The payoff

“Slow thinking, shipped software” is not a taste for slowness. It is a claim: that the fast, fluent, immediately gratifying output of a System 1 machine is exactly the thing that feels most finished and is most likely to be wrong in the ways that hurt later—and that the remedy is not a cleverer intuition but an external second system: written intent, deliberate checkpoints, and independent verification, applied where the stakes justify the cost.

That is what System2 is. Not a slower way to code. A deliberate one.

The thesis, in one line: a modern large language model is a magnificent System 1—and no amount of prompting turns a System 1 into a System 2. You have to build the second system around it.
